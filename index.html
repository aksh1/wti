“Democracy requires an informed citizenry and transparency of information which are vital to its functioning and also to contain corruption and to hold governments and their instrumentalities accountable to the governed”, says the preamble of the Indian Right to Information (RTI) Act.

Sounds confusing? Allow us to simplify it for you!


WHAT IS RIGHT TO INFORMATION ALL ABOUT?


Right to Information is an ingenious initiative taken by the Department of Personnel and Training, Ministry of Personnel, Public Grievances and Pensions in 2005. It is claimed as one of the World’s best law with an excellent implementation record. It mandates timely response to citizen requests for government information as well as a quick search of information on the details of various government authorities. The enactment of the Right to Information 2005 marks a significant shift for Indian democracy, for the greater access to citizens to information; the greater will be the responsiveness of government to community needs.

The rules of the Act are defined adequately. From the format for requisitioning information to the time period within which information must be provided; from the method of giving the information to the exemptions of information, all are outlined in the Act itself. Also the Act, in particular, requires every public authority to publish 16 categories of information. This includes the particulars of its organization, functions and duties; powers and duties of its officers and employees; procedure followed in the decision making process; norms set for discharge of its functions; rules, regulations, instructions, manuals and records, held by it or under its control or used by its employees for discharging its functions; etc. Most radical provision of the Act is that the information-seeker need not to give any reason for it or prove his locus standi.

In a nutshell, the RTI is not only a necessity of and for the citizens, but a condition to good governance. It gives the democracy a brighter color and empowers the citizens to actually participate in the governance process of the country.



RIGHT TO INFORMATION- NEED OF THE HOUR

It has taken 82 years for India to allow the opaque system of governance turn into transparent. The Act has been derived from Article 19 of the Constitution which is the fundamental right of freedom of speech and expression. If we do not have information on how our government and public institutions function, how will we able to form opinions and create the room for constructive criticism? The basic object of the Right to Information Act is to empower the citizens, promote transparency and accountability in the working of the Government, contain corruption, and make our democracy work for the people in real sense. It goes without saying that an informed citizen is better equipped to keep necessary vigil on the instruments of governance and make the government more accountable to the governed.
